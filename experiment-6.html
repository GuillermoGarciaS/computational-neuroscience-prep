Experiment #6 — Signal Integration and Interference

Experiment 5 treated representation as useful structure. This experiment asks what happens when several representations arrive at once, cooperate, conflict, or distort the final result.

Introduction

Central question

What happens when a system receives multiple signals at once?

Page thesis

More information is not automatically better

Multiple signals can reduce uncertainty, but they can also amplify noise, create false structure, or let one unreliable channel dominate the final representation.

Theory

A perception-like system rarely receives one clean signal. It may combine current sensory input, temporal context, memory, and expectation. Integration is useful when channels agree and are reliable. It becomes dangerous when confidence and reliability diverge.

Integrated score(state) = Σ channel_weight × channel_confidence

This page treats integration as an inference problem over hidden states: Pattern A, Pattern B, or Pattern C.

Interactive lab

Choose the true hidden state, tune three channels, and select an integration rule. The lab shows confidence, entropy change, conflict, dominant channel, and error.

Interpretation

The key lesson is that integration can both clarify and distort. Agreement among weak channels can beat one overconfident channel, but a bad integration rule can still choose the wrong state.

Limits

Main limitation

This model does not learn its integration rule. Real systems often adjust weights over time through feedback, attention, reward, and bodily context.

Core idea

Integration is not accumulation. It is rule-bound conflict management.

Warning

More signals can make a system worse when they are noisy or badly weighted.
