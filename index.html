From Entropy to Experience

A long-term portfolio of computational experiments on information, uncertainty, representation, and the limits of explaining subjective experience.

Core Question

To what extent can reducing entropy in computational systems explain structured representation and perception, and why does it fail to account for subjective experience?

Approach

This project adapts coursework into small computational studies. Each experiment focuses on a limited measurable problem, then asks what the result does and does not explain.

Scope

This is not a claim to explain consciousness. It is an attempt to map where computational explanation works, where it weakens, and where it appears to stop.

Experiments & Projects

Experiment 1 — File Size, Structure, and Predictability

A first study in how file size, repetition, and predictability relate without collapsing into meaning.

Experiment 2 — Noise, Speed, and Measurement

A comparison of measured performance under noise, variability, and changing technical conditions.

Experiment 3 — Variability Across Sources

An analysis of whether variation across sources is random or reflects deeper structural differences.

Experiment 4 — Stability, Repetition, and Comparison

A study of repeated measurements, stability through time, and the limits of comparing treatments.

Experiment 5 — Procedures, Constraints, and Output

An exploration of how different procedures and constraints change output form and efficiency.

Project 1 — Experimental Design and Inference

A foundational review of experimental design, hypothesis testing, and what statistical inference can justify.

Project 2 — Weather, Response Variables, and Model Limits

A model of precipitation as a response variable, used to examine prediction, factor choice, and explanatory limits.
