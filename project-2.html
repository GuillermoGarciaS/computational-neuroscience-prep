Project 2 — Multi-Channel Integration and Uncertainty

This synthesis asks what happens when a system receives several signals at once. Integration can reduce uncertainty, but only when the channels are useful rather than merely loud.

Introduction

Central question

When does combining multiple channels reduce uncertainty, and when does it create confusion?

Boundary

Abstract channels, not simulated senses

The channels in this page are not biological senses. They are simplified information streams with structure, noise, redundancy, and correlation.

Model

The model starts with a hidden source signal. Three channels observe it with different reliability. Integration combines the channels and compares the integrated output against the hidden source.

Integrated bit = weighted vote(channel A, channel B, channel C)

If the channels are redundant in a useful way, agreement increases confidence. If one channel is irrelevant or too noisy, integration can make the final output worse.

Uncertainty reduction = best single-channel error - integrated error

Interpretation

Integration helps when channels provide partially independent evidence about the same source. It hurts when agreement is low, noise is high, or one channel carries irrelevant information.

This points toward sensory integration and neural coding, but it remains abstract. It studies uncertainty in information streams, not biological perception directly.

Limits

Main limitation

The simulation assumes a hidden source and clean correctness metric. Real perception often lacks such a simple ground truth, and biological integration includes timing, attention, embodiment, and learning.

Core idea

More signals are not automatically better. Integration depends on reliability and relevance.

Warning

Redundancy can reinforce signal, but it can also reinforce error.
